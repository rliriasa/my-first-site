Samsung's HBM Moment: Why Nvidia and Tesla Are Finally Calling
Bullish · On Watch SSNLF · OTC · PT Pending Qualification
Published by LIRIA RESEARCH · Independent Equity Research

For nearly two years, Samsung Electronics has watched from the sidelines as SK Hynix captured the lion's share of the explosive High Bandwidth Memory market. The Korean semiconductor giant—historically the world's largest memory producer—found itself in the unfamiliar position of laggard, losing ground in the highest-margin, fastest-growing segment of the entire chip industry. That narrative may now be shifting in ways the market has yet to fully price.

Recent reports indicate that both Nvidia and Tesla have initiated substantive qualification discussions with Samsung for next-generation HBM products. While qualification is not certification—and certification is not volume orders—the mere fact that the two most demanding AI compute buyers are engaging with Samsung represents a potential inflection point. If Samsung can secure even partial qualification for HBM3E or HBM4, the competitive dynamics of the memory industry could shift dramatically.

We are placing Samsung Electronics on our active watchlist with a Bullish stance, contingent on specific milestones that would trigger full initiation. The current share price of $45.20 reflects deep skepticism about Samsung's ability to compete in premium memory—skepticism that could prove misplaced if qualification milestones are achieved in the coming quarters. This note frames the bull case, identifies the specific catalysts we're monitoring, and establishes the analytical framework for a potential rating upgrade.

The HBM market is projected to grow from approximately $16 billion in 2024 to an estimated $40-50 billion by 2027. Samsung's current share of roughly 15-20% versus SK Hynix's dominant 50%+ position represents both the problem and the opportunity. A shift of just 10 percentage points of market share could add $4-5 billion in high-margin revenue annually.
Market Context
The HBM Landscape: Understanding Samsung's Disadvantage

High Bandwidth Memory has emerged as the critical bottleneck technology enabling modern AI infrastructure. Unlike conventional DRAM, HBM stacks multiple memory dies vertically using through-silicon vias (TSVs), achieving bandwidth performance of approximately 1-1.2 TB/s for current-generation HBM3E products. This technology is essential for training large language models and running inference at scale, making it the memory type of choice for Nvidia's H100, H200, and forthcoming Blackwell architectures, as well as AMD's MI300 series.

SK Hynix moved first and moved decisively. The company achieved Nvidia qualification for HBM3 in early 2023 and has maintained its position as the primary supplier ever since. Industry estimates suggest SK Hynix captured approximately 50-55% of the HBM market in 2025, with Micron holding roughly 25-30% and Samsung relegated to an estimated 15-20% share. For a company accustomed to leading in memory technology, this represents an unprecedented competitive setback.

Samsung's HBM struggles have been primarily technical rather than strategic. Early HBM3 products reportedly suffered from thermal management issues that prevented Nvidia qualification. The company's 8-high stack configurations showed higher defect rates than competitors, and yield improvements lagged behind SK Hynix by an estimated 6-12 months. These are not insurmountable problems, but they have cost Samsung dearly in a market where first-mover advantage translates directly into locked-in customer relationships.

Catalyst Analysis
What Nvidia and Tesla Engagement Actually Means

The recent reports of Nvidia and Tesla engaging with Samsung for HBM supply deserve careful parsing. Engagement is not qualification. Qualification is not volume commitment. And volume commitment is not guaranteed margin preservation. However, the fact that these discussions are occurring at all signals several important developments.

First, Samsung appears to have resolved its most critical technical issues. Nvidia's qualification process is notoriously rigorous, involving months of thermal testing, reliability validation, and integration verification. The company would not waste engineering resources on a supplier whose products couldn't meet baseline specifications. That Nvidia is reportedly conducting active qualification testing suggests Samsung's HBM3E products have cleared initial screening—a meaningful technical milestone.

Second, supply chain diversification has become strategically urgent for hyperscalers. SK Hynix's dominance creates concentration risk that Nvidia, Tesla, and other AI infrastructure builders can no longer ignore. With AI capex projected to exceed $250-300 billion annually by 2027, the largest buyers have powerful incentives to cultivate alternative suppliers. Samsung's scale and manufacturing expertise make it the natural second source—if it can meet technical requirements.

Tesla's involvement is particularly noteworthy. The company's Dojo supercomputer project and its growing AI inference needs for Full Self-Driving require substantial HBM capacity. Tesla has historically demonstrated willingness to work with suppliers during qualification phases to accelerate development—a pattern that could benefit Samsung. Additionally, Tesla's vertical integration philosophy may make it more tolerant of early-generation supply arrangements than hyperscalers with more established vendor relationships.

Financial Framework
Quantifying the HBM Opportunity for Samsung

Samsung Electronics is a sprawling conglomerate with approximately $220-230 billion in annual revenue across semiconductors, displays, consumer electronics, and other segments. The Memory division—comprising DRAM and NAND—typically contributes roughly 25-30% of total revenue but a disproportionate share of operating profit due to its higher margins during upcycles. Understanding the HBM opportunity requires isolating this segment's dynamics.

HBM commands substantial premiums over conventional DRAM. Industry estimates suggest HBM3E pricing of approximately $10-15 per gigabyte, compared to $2-4/GB for standard DDR5. This 3-5x premium, combined with the technical complexity limiting supplier competition, creates margin profiles estimated at 40-50% gross margin for qualified HBM versus 25-35% for commodity DRAM. The strategic importance of HBM extends beyond its direct revenue contribution—it increasingly determines which suppliers win allocation in adjacent memory categories.

If Samsung can increase its HBM market share from the current estimated 17% to 25-30% by 2027, the incremental revenue impact could reach $5-8 billion annually at projected market sizes. More importantly, this would represent high-margin revenue that could meaningfully improve the Memory division's overall profitability. We estimate that each percentage point of HBM market share gain could add approximately $100-150 million in operating profit annually at current pricing levels.

Technical Assessment
Samsung's Path to HBM Competitiveness

Samsung's HBM challenges have been well-documented but are worth understanding in detail, as resolution of these issues is central to the bull thesis. The primary obstacles have been thermal performance, yield rates, and advanced packaging capabilities—each representing distinct technical domains.

Thermal management has been the most visible challenge. HBM stacks generate substantial heat, and early Samsung HBM3 products reportedly exhibited junction temperatures approximately 5-10°C higher than SK Hynix equivalents under comparable workloads. For Nvidia's datacenter GPUs, which already operate near thermal limits, this difference was disqualifying. Samsung has reportedly addressed this through redesigned thermal interface materials and improved heat spreader integration, though independent verification of these improvements remains limited.

Yield rates determine economic viability. HBM manufacturing requires stacking 8-12 dies with near-perfect alignment and TSV connectivity. Early Samsung 8-high stacks reportedly achieved yields in the 50-60% range versus SK Hynix yields estimated at 70-80%. This gap translates directly into cost disadvantages and capacity constraints. Samsung has invested heavily in advanced metrology and process control, with recent reports suggesting yield improvements to the 65-75% range—still below SK Hynix but potentially competitive for secondary qualification.

Advanced packaging represents both a challenge and an opportunity. Samsung's investment in its Foundry division's advanced packaging capabilities—including its I-Cube and X-Cube technologies—provides potential synergies for HBM integration that SK Hynix cannot replicate. If Samsung can demonstrate superior package-level thermal performance or enable tighter integration with logic dies, this could become a competitive differentiator rather than merely achieving parity.

Competitive Dynamics
The Three-Player HBM Market Structure

The HBM market's current structure—with SK Hynix dominant, Micron as qualified second source, and Samsung as aspirational third—creates specific competitive dynamics that inform our outlook. Understanding these dynamics helps assess the probability and magnitude of Samsung's potential share gains.

SK Hynix faces capacity constraints that create natural openings. Despite aggressive expansion, SK Hynix's HBM production capacity is estimated at approximately 150,000 wafers per month, with plans to reach 200,000+ by late 2027. However, demand projections suggest the market could absorb 300,000+ wafers monthly by 2027, implying structural undersupply even with all three major players at full capacity. This supply-demand imbalance means Samsung need not take share from SK Hynix directly—it can capture incremental demand that SK Hynix cannot serve.

Micron's position illustrates the qualified-second-source playbook. Micron achieved Nvidia qualification approximately 12-18 months after SK Hynix and has steadily grown its share from negligible levels to an estimated 25-30% today. The company did not need to match SK Hynix on every specification—it needed to meet minimum requirements and offer supply diversity value. Samsung can plausibly follow this path, accepting initially lower volumes and potentially lower pricing to establish presence, then expanding as it demonstrates reliability.

The transition to HBM4 may reset competitive positions. HBM4, expected to begin sampling in 2027 with volume production in 2028, represents a generational shift with new packaging approaches and potential architecture changes. All three suppliers are essentially starting from similar positions on HBM4 development. If Samsung can achieve technical parity on HBM4 while maintaining cost advantages from its larger overall manufacturing scale, the next-generation transition could enable more meaningful share gains than incremental HBM3E improvements.

Risk Assessment
Key Risks and Mitigating Factors
  • Qualification Failure or Delay

    The most significant risk is that Samsung fails to achieve Nvidia qualification for HBM3E, or that qualification is delayed beyond 2026. Each quarter of delay allows SK Hynix to further entrench its position and potentially lock in multi-year supply agreements. If Samsung cannot qualify for Blackwell-generation products, the opportunity window may close until HBM4.

    Mitigant: Tesla engagement provides an alternative qualification path. Additionally, AMD's MI400 series may offer qualification opportunities with less stringent requirements than Nvidia.

  • Margin Compression from Competitive Pricing

    Even if Samsung achieves qualification, it may need to offer significant price discounts to win volume. If Samsung HBM pricing comes in 15-25% below SK Hynix levels to compensate for perceived quality gaps, the margin benefit of HBM participation would be substantially reduced. This could make share gains less accretive than our base case assumes.

    Mitigant: Supply-demand imbalance in HBM provides pricing support. Additionally, Samsung's scale in conventional DRAM provides cost structure advantages that could support competitive pricing while maintaining margins.

  • AI Capex Deceleration

    The HBM market size projections underlying our thesis assume continued aggressive AI infrastructure investment. If AI spending growth decelerates due to monetization challenges, regulatory intervention, or technological shifts, the total addressable market could be substantially smaller than projected. This would reduce the absolute dollar opportunity even if Samsung gains share.

    Mitigant: Current hyperscaler capex guidance through 2027 remains robust. The installed base buildout has multi-year momentum regardless of near-term sentiment shifts.

  • Geopolitical and Supply Chain Disruptions

    Samsung's manufacturing concentration in South Korea creates geopolitical risk, though this applies equally to SK Hynix. More specifically, advanced packaging equipment sourced from ASML and other Western suppliers could face export restrictions that differentially impact Samsung's ramp plans.

    Mitigant: Samsung has diversified some advanced packaging capacity to its Texas facilities. Additionally, Korean suppliers are generally favored in current export control frameworks relative to Chinese competitors.

Initiation Framework
Milestones That Would Trigger Full Coverage

We are placing Samsung on our watchlist rather than initiating full coverage because the investment thesis hinges on upcoming binary events whose outcomes remain uncertain. Our framework identifies specific milestones that would trigger rating changes and price target establishment.

Upgrade to Buy with Price Target: Confirmed Nvidia qualification for HBM3E with volume supply agreement, OR Tesla Dojo qualification with meaningful capacity commitment, OR HBM market share evidence above 22% for two consecutive quarters. Any of these developments would demonstrate that Samsung has crossed the technical threshold necessary to compete in premium memory, justifying full initiation with a constructive price target likely in the $55-65 range.

Downgrade to Neutral: Failure to achieve any major qualification by Q4 2026, OR evidence of continued yield issues in HBM3E production, OR loss of conventional DRAM share indicating broader competitive deterioration. These developments would suggest that Samsung's HBM challenges reflect deeper organizational or technical problems rather than temporary setbacks.
